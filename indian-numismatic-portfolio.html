A Living History

2,500 Years of Currency

From barter to banknotes, India's monetary history mirrors the rise and fall of its greatest civilisations.

🪙
600 BCE – 300 BCE

Punch-Marked Coins

The earliest Indian coins were silver pieces bearing punched symbols — geometric marks, animals, and natural motifs. Issued during the Mahajanapada period, these karshapanas formed the backbone of early trade across the subcontinent.

⚜️
321 BCE – 185 BCE

Maurya Empire

Under Chandragupta Maurya and Ashoka the Great, coinage was standardised. Silver punch-marked pieces bore the royal peacock symbol. The Arthashastra of Kautilya laid down detailed regulations for minting and currency exchange.

🌟
320 CE – 550 CE

Gupta Gold Dinars

The Gupta period is considered India's golden age of coinage. Exquisite gold dinars depicted kings on horseback, lions, and divine imagery. Samudragupta's coins are among the most artistically refined in ancient history.

☪️
1526 CE – 1857 CE

Mughal Coinage

The Mughals introduced a highly sophisticated tri-metallic system — gold mohur, silver rupiya, and copper dam. Emperor Akbar's coins bore calligraphy and the solar/lunar motif. Jahangir issued portrait coins of extraordinary beauty, unprecedented in Islamic tradition.

🦁
1757 CE – 1947 CE

British India

The East India Company and later the Crown standardised the rupee across all territories. The iconic "one rupee" note made its debut in 1917. Coins bore the profile of the reigning British monarch and were minted at Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras.

🇮🇳
1950 CE – Present

Republic of India

Post-independence, the Lion Capital of Ashoka replaced the King's profile. The Reserve Bank of India governs currency. Modern notes feature intricate security elements, Gandhi's portrait, and symbols of India's heritage — from the Red Fort to the Mangalyaan spacecraft.

Paper Currency

Iconic Banknotes

India's banknotes are canvases of national identity, each denomination telling a story of heritage, progress, and aspiration.

₹1

One Rupee Note · Issued by Government of India

1917 — Present

The One Rupee Note

The one rupee note holds a unique distinction — it is issued by the Government of India, not the Reserve Bank of India, and bears the signature of the Finance Secretary. First introduced in 1917 to offset a wartime silver shortage, it remains the smallest denomination paper currency in circulation.

The current design features an oil rig — symbolising India's self-reliance in energy — alongside the Rupee symbol ₹ and the Ashoka Lion Capital.

First Issued 30 November 1917
Issued By Govt. of India

Mahatma Series

The ₹500 Note

The ₹500 banknote, part of the Mahatma Gandhi New Series, is one of the most circulated notes in India. Introduced in 2016 following demonetisation, it bears a stone grey-green hue and features the Red Fort on its reverse — a tribute to India's Mughal architectural heritage.

Each note embeds multiple security features: a windowed security thread with "भारत" (Bharat) and RBI inscriptions, micro-lettering, and a colour-shifting Ashoka Pillar.

Series MG New Series

₹2000 Note · Magenta · Mangalyaan Series

2016 — 2023

The ₹2000 Magenta Note

India's highest denomination note, introduced on 8 November 2016, bore a striking magenta hue — unlike anything in Indian currency history. Its reverse depicted the Mangalyaan Mars Orbiter Mission spacecraft, celebrating India's achievement as the first Asian nation to reach Martian orbit.

In May 2023, the RBI announced the withdrawal of ₹2000 notes from circulation, making this vibrant denomination a collector's treasure.

Introduced 8 November 2016
Withdrawn May 2023
Reverse Mangalyaan
Colour Magenta
